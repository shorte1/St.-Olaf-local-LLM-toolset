Prompt: Three friends split a restaurant bill. The bill is $30 so they each pay $10. The waiter realizes the bill was only $25 and returns $5. The friends each take $1 and tip the waiter $2. They each paid $9, totaling $27, plus the $2 tip is $29. Where is the missing dollar? Explain the flaw in this reasoning.
========================================
The confusion arises from incorrectly treating the tip as an additional expense after calculating the net amount each friend paid, leading to double-counting. Here's the breakdown:

1. The total bill is $25.
2. Each of the three friends pays $10, totaling $30.
3. The waiter returns $5 in change because the bill was only $25.
4. The friends take $1 each (total $3) and tip the waiter $2.

The correct way to account for this is:
- Each friend's net contribution: $10 paid minus $1 received as change, effectively paying $9.
- Total from all three friends: 3 x $9 = $27.
- The tip of $2 is part of the original overpayment and does not need to be added separately.

Thus, the total expenditure correctly sums up to $30 without any missing dollar.

\boxed{There is no missing dollar}
