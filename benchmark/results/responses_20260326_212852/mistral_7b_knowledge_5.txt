Prompt: Explain the difference between TCP and UDP protocols and when you would use each.
========================================
 TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) and UDP (User Datagram Protocol) are two primary transport layer protocols used in networking, particularly in Internet communications. Here's a brief comparison of the two:

1. **Connection-Oriented vs Connectionless:**
   - TCP is a connection-oriented protocol, which means it establishes a reliable and ordered full-duplex communication channel between the sender and receiver before data transfer begins. It uses three-way handshaking (SYN-SYN ACK-ACK) for establishing a connection and four-way handshaking (FIN-ACK) for closing the connection.
   - UDP, on the other hand, is a connectionless protocol. It doesn't establish a direct link between sender and receiver before data transmission. Instead, it sends individual datagrams (packets), which may or may not reach their destination.

2. **Reliability:**
   - TCP provides reliable data transfer by ensuring that all packets are delivered to the recipient in the same order they were sent and without loss or duplication. If a packet is lost, it will be retransmitted until it's successfully received. This comes at the cost of slower transmission due to the overhead involved in error checking and retransmission.
   - UDP does not guarantee packet delivery, order, or protection against duplicate packets. It simply sends datagrams without checking whether they were received correctly. This makes UDP faster than TCP since it doesn't need to spend time on error checking and retransmission.

3. **Applications:**
   - TCP is ideal for applications requiring guaranteed delivery of data, such as web browsing, file transfers, email services, and real-time communications (VoIP). The reliability of TCP ensures that all data sent by the server is received by the client without errors or duplicates.
   - UDP is suitable for applications where speed is more important than reliability, such as online gaming, streaming media, and DNS lookups. Since UDP doesn't have to spend time on error checking and retransmission, it can transmit data faster than TCP, which makes it useful in real-time applications where low latency is crucial.

In summary, you would use TCP for applications that require reliable data transfer and are willing to sacrifice some speed for this reliability (e.g., web browsing), while UDP is more appropriate for applications where speed is essential and occasional loss or delay of data can be tolerated (e.g., online gaming).
