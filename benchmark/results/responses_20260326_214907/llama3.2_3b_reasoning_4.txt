Prompt: Three friends split a restaurant bill. The bill is $30 so they each pay $10. The waiter realizes the bill was only $25 and returns $5. The friends each take $1 and tip the waiter $2. They each paid $9, totaling $27, plus the $2 tip is $29. Where is the missing dollar? Explain the flaw in this reasoning.
========================================
The "missing" dollar is actually not a missing dollar at all, but rather a misunderstanding of how the payment and tipping process works.

Here's what happened:

1. The bill was originally split among three friends, with each paying $10.
2. The waiter returns $5 to the restaurant because the bill was overcharged by $5.
3. Each friend takes their original $10 back ($5 returned) and pays the remaining amount of $5 on the bill (not $9).
4. They then take an additional $1 from each other, essentially dividing the $5 remaining among themselves again, to reach a new total of $7 for each friend.
5. The friends tip the waiter $2 each.

The correct calculation is:

3 friends x $10 each = $30 (initial amount paid)
$5 returned by waiter = $25 (new bill)
Each friend pays $7 ($5 remaining + $1 from others) to the waiter, totaling $21.
Adding the $6 in tips ($2 x 3) results in a total payment of $27.

So, there is no "missing" dollar. The calculation simply shows that the friends paid $27 and received $29 in change (the original $30 bill minus the overcharge of $5).
