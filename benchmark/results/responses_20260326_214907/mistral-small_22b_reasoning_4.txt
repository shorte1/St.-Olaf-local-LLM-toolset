Prompt: Three friends split a restaurant bill. The bill is $30 so they each pay $10. The waiter realizes the bill was only $25 and returns $5. The friends each take $1 and tip the waiter $2. They each paid $9, totaling $27, plus the $2 tip is $29. Where is the missing dollar? Explain the flaw in this reasoning.
========================================
 This problem can be more clearly understood by considering what happened to each friend's money individually:

- Initially, each of the three friends gave the waiter \$10. That's a total of \$30 given to the waiter.
- The bill was only \$25, so the waiter returned \$5. If we think about what happens to that \$5, it goes back into the hands of the friends in some way—it could be divided evenly among them or just handed to one person; for our purposes, it doesn't matter how it is distributed.
- The friends decide to leave a \$2 tip, so they need to come up with another \$2 that hasn't been accounted for yet. This means that the friends need to take back \$3 from what they originally gave to the waiter (the \$5 in returned change minus the \$2 in tip money).
- The important thing to realize here is that this \$3 could have come from any one or two of them, depending on how the original \$5 was split. However, since each friend ends up with \$1 less than he started with (\$9 instead of \$10), we know that the \$3 must have been taken from all three friends equally.
- So, in total, the waiter received \$25 (the bill) + \$2 (tip) = \$27 from the friends, and the friends left with \$27 (\$9 each * 3 friends)—for a total of \$54, which is exactly what they started with.

The key point to understand here is that when the waiter returns change, he doesn't have to return it equally or in the same way that it was given to him. The only thing we know for sure is how much money each friend has at the end of the interaction—and since they all end up with \$9, we can infer that they must have taken back a total of \$3 from the original amount given.
