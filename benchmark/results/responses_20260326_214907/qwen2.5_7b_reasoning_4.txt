Prompt: Three friends split a restaurant bill. The bill is $30 so they each pay $10. The waiter realizes the bill was only $25 and returns $5. The friends each take $1 and tip the waiter $2. They each paid $9, totaling $27, plus the $2 tip is $29. Where is the missing dollar? Explain the flaw in this reasoning.
========================================
This problem is a classic example of a mathematical riddle that plays with how numbers are perceived and combined. The key to understanding where the "missing dollar" supposedly went lies in carefully examining each part of the transaction.

Here's the step-by-step breakdown:

1. **Initial Payment**: 
   - Three friends each pay $10, totaling $30.
   
2. **Correct Bill**:
   - The actual bill is $25.
   - Therefore, a total refund of $5 should be given back to them.

3. **Return of Money**:
   - The waiter returns $5, with each friend getting $1 and keeping the remaining $2 as a tip for themselves.

4. **Money Tracked by Friends**:
   - Each friend paid $9 (initially) minus $1 (refund), so they effectively paid $8 each.
   - Therefore, \(3 \times 8 = 24\) dollars were actually paid to the restaurant.
   - Adding the $2 tip: \(24 + 2 = 26\).

The flaw in your reasoning is that you incorrectly combined these amounts. You shouldn't add the total payment ($27) and the tip ($2) because the $27 already includes the $2 tip, making it redundant.

Here’s the correct way to look at it:

- Each friend effectively paid $8 (initial $10 - $1 refund).
- The restaurant received a total of \(3 \times 8 = 24\) dollars.
- The waiter kept a $2 tip.

So, there is no missing dollar. The correct accounting is:
\[ \text{Total payment to the restaurant} + \text{Tip given to the waiter} = 26 \]

In summary, the initial $30 minus the $5 refund ($24 + $2) equals the correct total of what was paid and retained.

So, there is no missing dollar; it’s a matter of correctly accounting for each component of the transaction.
