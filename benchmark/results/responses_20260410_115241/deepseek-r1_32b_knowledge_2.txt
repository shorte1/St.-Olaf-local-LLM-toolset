Prompt: What is the CAP theorem in distributed systems and what are its practical implications?
========================================
The CAP theorem is a fundamental concept in distributed systems that states a system cannot simultaneously guarantee Consistency, Availability, and Partition Tolerance (CAP). Instead, it must prioritize two out of the three. Here's a structured summary:

1. **Consistency**: Ensures all nodes have the same data at any time. Critical for applications like banking where data accuracy is paramount.

2. **Availability**: Guarantees that requests receive timely responses, essential for user experience in web services and applications.

3. **Partition Tolerance**: Allows the system to handle network failures without compromising functionality.

**Implications:**
- **High Availability & Partition Tolerance (e.g., DynamoDB, Cassandra)**: Systems prioritize availability and partition tolerance, often using eventual consistency where data may lag but converges over time.
  
- **Consistency & Partition Tolerance (e.g., Two-Phase Commit)**: Used in applications like financial transactions, ensuring data integrity even if it means lower availability during network issues.

- **Trade-offs**: Engineers must balance these aspects based on application needs. For instance, social media might favor availability and partition tolerance, while healthcare systems prioritize consistency and partition tolerance.

**Conclusion**: The CAP theorem guides architects to make informed decisions, understanding that while all three can't be achieved simultaneously, strategic choices enhance system performance in critical areas. It's about balancing priorities and using mechanisms like eventual consistency or compensation during partitions to mitigate trade-offs.
