Prompt: Three friends split a restaurant bill. The bill is $30 so they each pay $10. The waiter realizes the bill was only $25 and returns $5. The friends each take $1 and tip the waiter $2. They each paid $9, totaling $27, plus the $2 tip is $29. Where is the missing dollar? Explain the flaw in this reasoning.
========================================
The three friends split a restaurant bill that initially was supposed to be $25, but they each paid $10, totaling $30. The waiter realized the bill was only $25 and returned $5, giving each friend $1 (so $1 back per person) and keeping $2 as a tip.

Each friend effectively paid $9 ($10 - $1). Therefore, the total amount paid by all three friends is $27 ($9 x 3). Adding the $2 tip to this $27 gives us $29. However, this is incorrect because the $2 tip was already included in the net payment of $27.

The key breakdown is as follows:
- Total bill: $25
- Amount initially paid by all three: $30
- Refund given back: $5 (so each friend gets $1)
- Tip given to waiter: $2

When considering the effective payment after refunds, each friend has paid $9. The total of these payments is $27, which includes both the bill ($25) and the tip ($2). Therefore, adding the tip again results in an overcount.

The correct accounting shows that there is no missing dollar because all amounts are properly accounted for:
- Total payment: $30
- Refund given back: $5
- Net payment after refund: $27 (which covers the bill and the tip)

Thus, the final answer is:

\boxed{There is no missing dollar; the apparent discrepancy arises from improper accounting of the tips and refunds.}
