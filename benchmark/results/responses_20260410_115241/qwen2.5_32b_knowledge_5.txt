Prompt: Explain the difference between TCP and UDP protocols and when you would use each.
========================================
TCP (Transmission Control Protocol) and UDP (User Datagram Protocol) are both transport layer protocols used in network communications to send data over the internet or other IP networks, but they operate differently and are suited for different types of applications.

### TCP (Transmission Control Protocol)
- **Connection-Oriented:** Before any actual data can be sent, a connection needs to be established using a three-way handshake.
- **Reliable Delivery:** TCP ensures that all packets are delivered in the correct order. It uses acknowledgments and retransmissions to guarantee delivery.
- **Flow Control:** TCP adjusts its transmission rate based on network conditions to avoid overwhelming the receiver with too much data at once.
- **Error Checking:** Each packet sent through TCP includes a checksum to ensure the integrity of the data.
- **Use Cases:** Applications that require reliable, ordered delivery such as web browsing (HTTP/HTTPS), file transfers (FTP/SFTP), and email (SMTP) use TCP.

### UDP (User Datagram Protocol)
- **Connectionless:** Unlike TCP, there's no need for a connection to be established before sending data. Packets can be sent immediately.
- **Unreliable Delivery:** No guarantees are provided regarding the order or arrival of packets; some may get lost without being retransmitted.
- **No Flow Control:** The sender does not adjust its rate based on network conditions, which means it could flood the receiver with too much data at once if there's no external control.
- **Less Overhead:** Due to fewer checks and processes compared to TCP, UDP is faster but less reliable.
- **Use Cases:** Real-time applications like online gaming, video streaming, VoIP (Voice over IP), and DNS queries benefit from the speed of UDP as they can handle some packet loss better than delays.

### Choosing Between TCP and UDP
The choice between TCP and UDP depends on the specific requirements of your application:

- **TCP** is typically used when reliability and order are more important than speed. This includes applications where data integrity and completeness are crucial, such as financial transactions or sensitive communications.
  
- **UDP** is preferred for real-time applications where low latency is critical, even if it means some data loss might occur. Examples include live video streaming services like YouTube Live or Twitch, where a slight delay in receiving packets can be more detrimental than missing out on occasional frames.

In summary, the decision between TCP and UDP should be based on whether your application prioritizes reliability (TCP) over speed (UDP).
